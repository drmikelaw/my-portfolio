Biography

Academic & Professional Background

Dr. Mike Law serves as Clinical Associate Professor and Graduate Program Coordinator for the M.S. in Instructional Design & Technology program at Georgia State University. His career represents a rare fusion of deep industry practice and scholarly rigor.

With more than 25 years of experience designing, implementing, and evaluating learning solutions in Fortune 500 environments, Dr. Law brings an unmatched practitioner lens to his academic role. He has led enterprise-scale learning initiatives across industries including retail, logistics, aviation, public health, and consumer products.

At GSU, Dr. Law oversees curriculum development, faculty coordination, and the Corporate Learning Advisory Board — an industry partnership model that ensures students graduate with skills directly aligned with employer needs. His teaching emphasizes the application of evidence-based instructional design theory to solve real organizational performance challenges.

Dr. Law currently serves as Vice President of Technology for the ISPI Atlanta Chapter, contributing to the professional development community for human performance improvement practitioners across the region.

Teaching Philosophy

Bridging Theory and Practice

"Instructional designers are, at their core, performance consultants. The most effective practitioners understand not just how people learn, but how organizations function — and how to bridge the two."

My teaching philosophy is rooted in the scholar-practitioner model. I believe that rigorous academic theory and real-world practice are not in tension — they are mutually reinforcing. The best instructional designers emerge from programs that challenge them to think critically about evidence while simultaneously applying that knowledge to authentic problems.

In every course I teach, students engage with real clients, real data, and real organizational challenges. This is not simulation — it is supervised practice, grounded in the theoretical frameworks that give practitioners the language and logic to explain what they do and why it works.

Core Principles

Performance-Centered Design

Learning interventions must be evaluated against measurable performance outcomes, not just learner satisfaction or knowledge acquisition.

Authentic Learning Contexts

Students learn best when they grapple with real organizational problems, not hypothetical scenarios. Industry partnerships make this possible.

Evidence-Based Practice

Design decisions must be traceable to evidence — from the needs analysis through evaluation. Intuition is a starting point, not a justification.
